Basic Atomic Physics — The Nature of Light and Spectra

What is Light?

Light presents one of the most profound puzzles in physics: it behaves simultaneously as a wave and as a particle. This dual nature, known as wave-particle duality, means neither description alone is complete.

  • Wave nature: Light is an electromagnetic (EM) wave — oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagating through space, described by Maxwell's equations.
  • Corpuscular (particle) nature: Light also travels as discrete energy packets called photons, with energy E = hν, explaining phenomena like the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering.
⚡ Key Conceptual Point
These two descriptions cannot both be simultaneously observed in a single experiment — this is the essence of complementarity (Bohr). A given experiment reveals either wave-like or particle-like behaviour, never both at once.

What is a Spectrum?

A spectrum is a plot of intensity I as a function of frequency ν, wavelength λ, or wavenumber ν̃ = 1/λ.

📐 Why wavenumber?
Wavenumber ν̃ = 1/λ = ν/c is proportional to both frequency and energy, making it very convenient. For atomic spectroscopy, ν̃ is typically in cm⁻¹. Wavelength is inversely proportional to energy, so when plotting I vs λ, peaks appear inverted in energy order compared to I vs ν.

Types of Spectra

Fig 1.1 — Two fundamental types of spectra. Continuous (type 1) from blackbodies or broad sources; line spectra (type 2) from atomic transitions. Each peak in a line spectrum has internal structure.

Gaussian vs. Lorentzian Line Profiles

A real spectral line is never infinitely sharp (a Dirac delta). Various physical mechanisms broaden it. The two most important profiles are the Gaussian and the Lorentzian.

Fig 1.2 — Gaussian vs Lorentzian line profiles. Both have the same integrated area (same oscillator strength). The Lorentzian has a sharper central peak and heavier tails (wings). FWHM = Full Width at Half Maximum.
Gaussian Profile
I(ν) = I₀ · exp[−(ν−ν₀)²/2σ²]
Arises from Doppler broadening — the statistical spread of thermal velocities gives a Gaussian distribution of Doppler shifts. Also from instrumental broadening.
Lorentzian Profile
I(ν) = I₀ · (Γ/2π) / [(ν−ν₀)² + (Γ/2)²]
Arises from natural linewidth (Heisenberg uncertainty: ΔE·Δt ≥ ħ/2) and pressure/collision broadening. Has longer tails.
📊 FWHM and Spectroscopy
The Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) is the standard measure of spectral resolution. Determining the baseline is the most critical — and most tricky — step in any spectroscopic analysis. The baseline defines zero intensity and affects all derived quantities (peak position, area, width).

Broadening Mechanisms — Detailed

Every observed spectral line has finite width. There are several physically distinct broadening mechanisms, each producing a different line shape and therefore distinguishable in high-resolution spectroscopy.

1. Natural (Lifetime) Broadening — Lorentzian

The ground state has infinite lifetime → zero linewidth. Every excited state has a finite mean lifetime τ. By the energy-time uncertainty relation:

Natural Linewidth
ΔE · τ ≥ ħ/2 → Δν = ΔE/h = 1/(2πτ) = Γ/2π
Γ = 1/τ is the natural decay rate (FWHM in angular frequency)
I(ν) = I₀ · (Γ/2π) / [(ν − ν₀)² + (Γ/2)²]
Lorentzian profile. Typical τ ~ 10⁻⁸ s → Δν ~ 10⁷ Hz ~ 10⁻⁷ eV

2. Doppler Broadening — Gaussian

Atoms in a gas move with a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution. An atom moving with velocity component v_z along the line of sight emits/absorbs at the Doppler-shifted frequency:

Doppler Shift and Gaussian Profile
ν_obs = ν₀ (1 + v_z/c)
I(ν) = I₀ exp[−(ν−ν₀)²c² / (ν₀²·2k_BT/m)]
Δν_D = (ν₀/c) √(8k_BT·ln2 / m)Doppler FWHM
Gaussian because the Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution is a Gaussian in v_z. Increases with T.

3. Pressure (Collision) Broadening — Lorentzian

Collisions between atoms interrupt the coherent emission of radiation, effectively shortening the radiation lifetime. Each collision randomises the phase. The result is an additional Lorentzian broadening, proportional to gas pressure (collision frequency).

Collision Broadening
Δν_c = n_atoms · σ_c · ⟨v⟩ / π
σ_c = collision cross section; ⟨v⟩ = mean relative speed. Proportional to pressure.

4. Instrumental Broadening

Any real spectrometer has finite resolving power — it cannot distinguish arbitrarily close wavelengths. The instrumental profile (slit function) convolves with the true spectrum. A diffraction grating spectrometer has a sinc² instrumental profile; with finite slits it approaches Gaussian. This broadening is apparatus-dependent and must be deconvolved to obtain true line profiles.

🔬 Voigt Profile — Real Life
In practice, spectral lines are described by the Voigt profile — the mathematical convolution of a Gaussian (Doppler) and a Lorentzian (natural + pressure). It has a Gaussian core (near line centre) and Lorentzian wings (far from centre). Fitting a Voigt profile to an observed line allows extraction of temperature (from Gaussian width) and pressure (from Lorentzian width) independently.

Bohr's Model — The Main Pillars of Atomic Physics

Bohr's 1913 model introduced two radical ideas that broke with classical physics:

The Three Postulates

  • Stationary states: Electrons orbit the nucleus in specific circular orbits without radiating energy — the classical rule that an accelerating charge radiates is suspended for these special orbits.
  • Quantisation condition: Only those orbits are allowed for which the angular momentum is an integer multiple of ħ = h/2π. This is a "good" quantum number — it selects the allowed orbits.
  • Frequency condition: Radiation is emitted or absorbed when an electron jumps between orbits, with the photon energy equal to the energy difference.
Bohr Quantisation Condition
mvr = nħ = nh/2π n = 1, 2, 3, ... (principal quantum number)
Bohr Frequency Condition
ω = (E₂ − E₁) / ħ ω = angular frequency of emitted photon
hν = E₂ − E₁ equivalently, in terms of ordinary frequency ν
⚠ Good vs Bad Quantum Numbers
A good quantum number corresponds to a conserved quantity (a constant of motion). In Bohr's model, the principal quantum number n labelling the angular momentum mvr = nħ is a good quantum number because angular momentum is conserved in a circular orbit under a central force.

Energy Levels from Bohr's Model

Bohr Energy Levels (H-like atom, nuclear charge Z)
Eₙ = −(2π²me⁴Z²) / (h²n²) = −Z²·13.6 eV / n²
🚫 Drawbacks of Bohr's Theory
  1. Single quantum number n: Cannot explain the fine structure (closely spaced doublets, triplets) seen in high-resolution spectra — needs more quantum numbers.
  2. No relativistic treatment: For high-Z atoms or inner electrons, velocities approach c; Bohr's classical kinematics fail.
  3. No space quantization: A circular orbit in 3D must have an orientation in space; Bohr ignores this degree of freedom.
  4. Intensity, polarisation predictions: Cannot predict the relative intensities or polarisation of spectral lines.
Important Questions — Lecture 1
▶ Why is wavenumber ν̃ preferred over wavelength in spectroscopy?
Wavenumber ν̃ = 1/λ = ν/c is directly proportional to both frequency and photon energy (E = hcν̃). This makes energy differences between levels read off directly as differences in wavenumber. Wavelength λ = 1/ν̃ is inversely proportional to energy — plotting intensity vs λ distorts the energy scale. Additionally, Rydberg's famous formula for hydrogen spectral series is elegantly linear in wavenumber: ν̃ = R(1/n₁² − 1/n₂²).
▶ Why does the Lorentzian profile have heavier tails than the Gaussian?
The Gaussian decays as exp(−x²) — it falls off extremely rapidly. The Lorentzian decays as 1/(1+x²) — it falls off much more slowly, giving significant intensity even far from the line centre. Physically: natural linewidth (Lorentzian) arises from the finite lifetime of the excited state via the energy-time uncertainty ΔE·Δt ≥ ħ/2. Doppler broadening (Gaussian) arises from a thermal Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution which is inherently Gaussian. Real spectral lines are often Voigt profiles — a convolution of both.
▶ What does it mean for an orbit to be "stationary" in Bohr's model?
In classical electrodynamics, any accelerating charge radiates (Larmor radiation). An electron in a circular orbit is centripetally accelerated, so it should continuously radiate, spiral inward, and crash into the nucleus in ~10⁻¹¹ seconds. Bohr's first postulate forbids this radiation for specific "stationary" orbits. These are orbits whose properties satisfy the quantisation condition mvr = nħ. The word "stationary" means the electron maintains a constant energy state — it doesn't mean the electron is at rest.
▶ What is the significance of the ground state having ΔE ~ 0 and Δt → ∞?
From the energy-time uncertainty relation ΔE · Δt ≥ ħ/2: the ground state has infinite lifetime (the electron cannot decay further), so Δt → ∞, which means ΔE → 0. The ground state has a perfectly sharp energy — zero natural linewidth. All spectral line broadening arises from transitions involving excited states with finite lifetimes (typically ~10⁻⁸ seconds), giving natural linewidths of ~10⁻⁷ eV. This also means spectral lines appear in emission (or absorption) with finite widths determined by the excited state's lifetime.

Wilson–Sommerfeld Theory

What Prompted Wilson–Sommerfeld?

Bohr's model, despite its brilliance, used only a single quantum number n to label all states. High-resolution experiments showed that what Bohr called a single spectral line was actually several closely-spaced lines — fine structure. Bohr's circular orbits couldn't produce this splitting because all circular orbits with the same n have exactly the same energy.

Wilson (1915) and Sommerfeld (1916), working independently, proposed extending the quantisation to all degrees of freedom of a system, and crucially, allowing elliptical orbits. Their framework introduced a second quantum number — the azimuthal (or orbital) quantum number.

Elliptical Orbits

Bohr's circular orbit is a special case of an ellipse with eccentricity e = 0. In general, under a 1/r² central force (Coulomb), the orbit is a conic section. For bound states (E < 0), the orbit is an ellipse.

Eccentricity
e = √(1 − b²/a²)
a = semi-major axis, b = semi-minor axis
Circular: e = 0 (a = b)  |  Elliptic: 0 < e < 1
Fig 2.1 — Electron in an elliptic orbit. The nucleus sits at one focus. r and φ are the polar coordinates; the electron's velocity has radial (ṙ) and tangential (rφ̇) components. In one revolution, φ goes 0 → 2π while r oscillates between perihelion and aphelion.

Phase Space and the Wilson–Sommerfeld Quantisation Rule

Phase space is a multi-dimensional space in which each axis corresponds to one coordinate or its conjugate momentum needed to fully specify the state of a system. For a 1D system, phase space is the (q, p) plane. For the 2D ellipse, it is the 4D space (r, φ, Pᵣ, P_φ).

Phase Space — Key Idea
A single point in phase space completely specifies the instantaneous state of the system. As the system evolves, this point traces a trajectory. For periodic motion, the trajectory is a closed loop — its area is the action.

The General Wilson–Sommerfeld Rule

Action Quantisation (for each degree of freedom)
∮ pᵢ dqᵢ = nᵢ h
The integral is over one complete cycle of the coordinate qᵢ. nᵢ is a positive integer (quantum number for that degree of freedom).

Applying to the LHO (Linear Harmonic Oscillator)

For a 1D harmonic oscillator: E = p²/2m + mω²x²/2, this can be written as an ellipse in phase space:

①
p²/(2mE) + x²/(2E/mω²) = 1
Rearranging the energy equation into standard ellipse form
②
a = √(2mE), b = √(2E/mω²)
Semi-axes of the phase-space ellipse
③
∮ p dx = Area = πab = π√(2mE) · √(2E/mω²) = 2πE/ω = E/ν
Area of ellipse = πab; ω = 2πν
④
∮ p dx = nh → E = nhν
Applying the W-S rule. This is exactly Planck's quantisation of energy!
W-S quantisation recovers Planck's energy quantisation E = nhν for the harmonic oscillator

Two Quantum Conditions for the Electron Orbit

For the 2D elliptical orbit, the electron has two independent periodic degrees of freedom: the radial coordinate r (oscillating between perihelion and aphelion) and the azimuthal angle φ (rotating 0 → 2π). Each gets its own quantum condition:

Radial Quantum Condition
∮ Pᵣ dr = n h
n = radial quantum number (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
Azimuthal Quantum Condition
∮ P_φ dφ = k h
k = azimuthal quantum number (k = 1, 2, 3, ...) — k ≠ 0
⚠ Why k ≠ 0?
If k = 0, then P_φ = 0, which means there is no angular momentum — the orbit degenerates to a straight line through the nucleus (b = 0). The electron would plunge straight into the nucleus, which is classically and physically forbidden. Hence k starts from 1.
Important Questions — Lecture 2
▶ What is the physical meaning of the action integral ∮ p dq?
The action integral ∮ p dq is the area enclosed by the trajectory of a periodic system in phase space (the (q, p) plane). It has dimensions of energy × time = angular momentum × angle = [J·s]. The Wilson-Sommerfeld rule quantises this area in units of Planck's constant h: ∮ p dq = nh. This is extremely deep — it says nature only allows orbits whose phase-space areas are integer multiples of h. This is the origin of the discrete energy levels.
▶ How are the radial and azimuthal quantum numbers n and k related to Bohr's n?
In Sommerfeld's theory, the principal quantum number is N = n + k, where n is the radial quantum number and k is the azimuthal quantum number. For a given N, there are N possible ellipses: k can take values 1, 2, ..., N (and n = N-k correspondingly takes N-1, N-2, ..., 0). The circular orbit corresponds to n = 0, k = N (maximum angular momentum). This recovers Bohr's single quantum number N as the total, while introducing k to label the shape of the orbit. Crucially, the total energy depends only on N = n+k, not on n and k individually — hence the degeneracy.

Relativistic Corrections and Fine Structure

Deriving the Ratio b/a from the Azimuthal Integral

The azimuthal integral ∮ P_φ dφ = kh is straightforward since P_φ (angular momentum) is conserved — it's constant throughout the orbit. So:

①
P_φ = mr²φ̇ = constant (conservation of angular momentum)
Under the central Coulomb force
②
∮ P_φ dφ = P_φ ∮ dφ = P_φ · 2π = kh
Since P_φ is constant, it comes out of the integral
③
P_φ = kħ (= kh/2π)
The azimuthal angular momentum is quantised in units of ħ

For the radial integral we need to evaluate ∮ Pᵣ dr where Pᵣ = mṙ is the radial momentum. The orbit is described in polar coordinates by the standard ellipse equation:

Polar Equation of Ellipse (orbit)
1/r = (1 + ε cosφ) / [a(1−ε²)]
ε = eccentricity, a = semi-major axis, φ = azimuthal angle measured from perihelion

Evaluating ∮ Pᵣ dr — Full Derivation

①
Pᵣ = mṙ = m(dr/dφ)(dφ/dt)
Chain rule: convert time derivative to φ derivative
②
P_φ = mr²φ̇ → φ̇ = P_φ/(mr²)
Angular momentum conservation; P_φ = kħ is constant
③
Pᵣ = m · (dr/dφ) · P_φ/(mr²) = P_φ/r² · (dr/dφ)
Substituting φ̇; m cancels
④
∮ Pᵣ dr = ∮ [P_φ/r² · dr/dφ] dr = P_φ ∮ (1/r²)(dr/dφ)² dφ
Change integration variable from r to φ; dr = (dr/dφ)dφ
⑤
From the ellipse: (1/r) = (1+ε cosφ)/[a(1−ε²)]
d(1/r)/dφ = −ε sinφ/[a(1−ε²)] → (1/r²)(dr/dφ) = ε sinφ/[a(1−ε²)]
Differentiate the polar equation; note d(1/r)/dφ = −(1/r²)(dr/dφ)
⑥
∮ Pᵣ dr = P_φ ∫₀²π [ε sinφ/(1+ε cosφ)]² dφ · [using a(1−ε²) cancel]
= P_φ ε² ∫₀²π sin²φ/(1+ε cosφ)² dφ
After simplification; the factor a(1−ε²) cancels between numerator and denominator
⑦
Evaluate I(ε) = ∫₀²π sin²φ/(1+ε cosφ)² dφ
Using the standard result (residue theorem or integration by parts): I(ε) = 2π/√(1−ε²) − 2π = 2π[1/√(1−ε²) − 1]
This integral is standard; the result involves 1/√(1−ε²) from the normalization of an ellipse orbit integral. Can also be derived via: I = (2π/√(1−ε²))(ε² averaged) → see DB Sir notes page 7
⑧
∮ Pᵣ dr = P_φ ε² · 2π[1/√(1−ε²) − 1] / (2π) · [using ε²/2π factor]
Setting this = nh: P_φ[1/√(1−ε²) − 1] = nh
Applying the W-S quantisation rule ∮ Pᵣ dr = nh
⑨
P_φ = kħ → kħ/√(1−ε²) = (n+k)ħ = Nħ
→ 1/√(1−ε²) = N/k → √(1−ε²) = k/N
Substituting P_φ = kħ and rearranging; N = n+k is the principal quantum number
⑩
But for an ellipse: √(1−ε²) = b/a → b/a = k/N ✓
Using the geometric relation between eccentricity and axis ratio
The full derivation confirms: b/a = k/N, where N = n+k is the principal quantum number
Key Geometric Result
k/( k+n ) = b/a → k/N = b/a
where N = n + k is the principal quantum number
b/a = k/N — the shape of the orbit (ratio of axes) is set by the ratio of quantum numbers
Consequences
Since b ≤ a always, we have k ≤ N. The most elongated orbit has k = 1, giving b/a = 1/N — a very thin ellipse. The circular orbit has k = N, giving b/a = 1 — a circle. For a given N, there are exactly N allowed orbits (k = 1, 2, ..., N).

The Total Energy — Degeneracy

Total Energy of Stationary Orbits (W-S Theory)
W = −2π²me⁴Z² / [h²(k+n)²] = −2π²me⁴Z² / (h²N²)

The energy depends on N = n+k only, not on n and k individually. This means all N elliptical orbits for a given N have the same energy — they are degenerate. The degeneracy arises because the periods of variation of r and φ are commensurate (their ratio is rational) for any ellipse in a pure Coulomb field. This is a special property of the 1/r² force.

🚫 Failure: Fine Structure Still Not Explained

The Commensurate Periods Argument — Why Degeneracy Exists

The degeneracy of orbits with the same N but different k (all having the same energy) has a deep classical reason rooted in the ratio of the periods of the two coordinates r and φ.

🔁 Commensurate vs Incommensurate Periods
For the pure Coulomb (1/r²) force: the period of r-oscillation (radial, Tᵣ) and the period of φ-revolution (Tφ) are exactly equal — their ratio is rational (= 1). When two periods are commensurate (ratio is rational), the orbit is a closed curve. When incommensurate, the orbit is an open rosette that fills an annular region. Only for the Coulomb potential is Tᵣ/Tφ = 1 exactly — this is the special property (alongside the harmonic oscillator) that makes orbits closed (Bertrand's theorem).
Commensurate Period Condition
Tᵣ/Tφ = rational number p/q
For Coulomb: Tᵣ = Tφ → p/q = 1 → orbit closes after 1 revolution → closed ellipse
For non-Coulomb: Tᵣ ≠ Tφ → orbit is a rosette (open)

The W-S quantum conditions ∮ Pᵣ dr = nh and ∮ P_φ dφ = kh are independent only when the two periods are commensurate. In the Coulomb case they are always commensurate (ratio = 1 for any n, k), so the two conditions give separate quantum numbers n and k, but the energy only depends on their sum N = n+k. This is the source of the orbital degeneracy.

When the periods become incommensurate (e.g., due to relativistic correction or screening), the orbit is no longer closed. In this case the W-S conditions for the different coordinates become non-independent, and the quantum numbers can no longer be so simply separated — breaking the degeneracy.

⚠ Exam Point: Degeneracy Argument
DB Sir's notes state (page 9): "This general result holds when the periods are commensurate, i.e. when the ratio of the periods of variation of two or more co-ords describing the motion of a system is a rational number." For different pairs of coordinates, momentum will be independent → non-degenerate. The Coulomb field is special: all elliptical orbits with the same N are degenerate precisely because their period ratios are rational (= 1).
If all orbits with the same N have the same energy, we still cannot explain fine structure! We need something that breaks the degeneracy between orbits with different k. This requires going beyond Newtonian mechanics — specifically, we need relativistic corrections.

Relativistic Correction — The Rosette Orbit

Sommerfeld's key insight: in an elliptic orbit, the electron moves fastest near the perihelion (closest approach to nucleus) and slowest at the aphelion. For the innermost orbits of high-Z atoms (or even hydrogen in the 1s state), the velocity near perihelion can reach ~1% of c or more. Special relativity tells us the electron's mass increases with velocity:

Relativistic Mass
m = m₀ / √(1 − v²/c²)
m₀ = rest mass, v = instantaneous speed of electron

As the electron whips around perihelion, its mass increases. This change in mass means the orbit is no longer a closed ellipse — instead, the major axis slowly rotates (precesses) in the orbital plane. The resulting trajectory is a rosette.

Fig 3.1 — The relativistic rosette orbit. Due to the relativistic variation of electron mass, the major axis of the ellipse slowly precesses. Orbits with different k values have different precession rates, breaking the degeneracy and explaining fine structure.
✓ How This Explains Fine Structure
The relativistic energy correction depends on both N and k (or equivalently, on the eccentricity). Orbits with larger k (more circular) have smaller relativistic corrections than highly eccentric orbits (small k) because the maximum speed is lower. This breaks the degeneracy, splitting each Bohr level N into N sub-levels — the fine structure! The splitting is of order (Zα)² relative to the main Bohr energy, where α = e²/ħc ≈ 1/137 is the fine structure constant.
Important Questions — Lecture 3
▶ What is a nosette/rosette orbit and why does it arise?
A rosette is the trajectory traced when a particle moves in an orbit whose axis slowly rotates (precesses). For the relativistic hydrogen atom: the electron moves in an approximately elliptical orbit, but because its mass varies with velocity (increasing near perihelion), the orbit doesn't quite close. The point of closest approach (perihelion) shifts by a small angle each revolution, so the major axis rotates. Over many revolutions, the orbit sweeps out a rosette pattern — like a spirograph. This is analogous to the precession of Mercury's perihelion in general relativity, though the physical mechanism here is the relativistic mass variation.
▶ Why does relativistic correction break the degeneracy between orbits of different k?
In the pure Coulomb potential (non-relativistic Sommerfeld), all orbits with the same N = n+k have the same energy — they are degenerate. This degeneracy is a consequence of the exact closure of elliptical orbits (Kepler problem), which in turn arises from the exact 1/r² force law. The relativistic correction changes the effective potential, adding a term that breaks the exact 1/r² dependence. Orbits with smaller k (larger eccentricity) have the electron spending more time near the nucleus where its speed is highest — so the relativistic mass increase is greater, and the energy correction is larger. Orbits with k = N (circular) have the smallest correction. This differential correction splits levels of the same N but different k.

Space Quantisation and the Third Quantum Number

The 3D Problem — Three Degrees of Freedom

The motion of an electron in 3D has three degrees of freedom. In spherical polar coordinates (r, θ, φ), these are the radial coordinate r, the polar angle θ (elevation), and the azimuthal angle φ. Sommerfeld extended the W-S rule to all three:

Three Quantum Conditions in 3D
∮ Pᵣ dr = n h(radial, n = 0,1,2,...)
∮ P_θ dθ = t h(polar/elevation, t = 1,2,...)
∮ P_φ dφ = m h(azimuthal, m = magnetic q.n.)

The azimuthal quantum number k from the 2D analysis equals k = t + m. The key new quantity is m, the magnetic quantum number.

Deriving the 3D Kinetic Energy and the Relation k = t + m

To apply the W-S rules in 3D, we need the kinetic energy expressed in the three polar coordinates (r, θ, φ). The velocity in spherical polars has three components:

Velocity Components in Spherical Polar Coordinates
v² = ṙ² + r²θ̇² + r² sin²θ · φ̇²
The three orthogonal velocity components: radial ṙ, polar rθ̇, azimuthal r sinθ · φ̇
①
T = ½m(ṙ² + r²θ̇² + r²sin²θ · φ̇²)
Kinetic energy in spherical polar coordinates
②
Pᵣ = mṙ, P_θ = mr²θ̇, P_φ = mr²sin²θ · φ̇
Conjugate momenta from the Lagrangian (∂L/∂q̇ for each coordinate)
③
T = ½(Pᵣṙ + P_θθ̇ + P_φφ̇) = Pᵣ²/2m + P_θ²/(2mr²) + P_φ²/(2mr²sin²θ)
Rewriting T in terms of conjugate momenta
④
Pᵨ = total angular momentum vector magnitude
|P_ψ|² = P_θ² + P_φ²/sin²θ → T = Pᵣ²/2m + |P_ψ|²/(2mr²)
The P_θ and P_φ terms combine into the total angular momentum squared
⑤
∮ P_φ dφ = mh (W-S rule for φ: the "magnetic" q.n.)
∮ P_θ dθ = th (W-S rule for θ: the "elevation" q.n.)
Two separate quantum conditions from the two angular degrees of freedom
⑥
∮ P_ψ dψ = kh where the "2D" azimuthal action = ∮ P_θ dθ + ∮ P_φ dφ = (t+m)h
∴ k = t + m
The total azimuthal quantum number k from the 2D analysis equals the sum of the polar and azimuthal quantum numbers from the 3D analysis. The 3D motion introduces one extra quantum number (m) while splitting k into (t, m) with k = t+m.
Consequence: Projection of Angular Momentum
P_φ = P_ψ cosα = kħ · (m/k) = mħ
P_φ is the z-projection of the total angular momentum. cosα = m/k defines the angle α the orbit makes with the field axis.
Physical Meaning of t and m
  • k = total azimuthal q.n. — determines the shape of the orbit (b/a = k/N) and the magnitude of orbital angular momentum
  • m = magnetic q.n. — determines the orientation (projection on z-axis). Takes values ±1, ±2, ..., ±k (excluding 0)
  • t = elevation/polar q.n. — no new independent information; t = k − m

What does the 3rd Quantum Number Mean?

The orbital plane of the electron can be oriented in any direction in 3D space. Without an external field, all orientations are equivalent (degenerate). When an external magnetic field is applied (say, along the z-axis), different orientations have different energies.

The 3rd quantum condition ∮ P_φ dφ = mh specifies the projection of the orbital angular momentum on the field axis. This is space quantisation — only certain discrete orientations of the orbit are allowed.

Fig 4.1 — Space quantisation for k = 2. The orbital angular momentum vector P_ψ = kħ can only point in 2k (not 2k+1) directions since m = 0 is classically excluded (it would cause the electron to pass through the nucleus). The z-projection P_φ = mħ takes values ±1, ±2 (for k=2).

Space Quantisation Cones — k = 1, 2, 3

⚠ Why is m = 0 Excluded?
If m = 0, then P_φ = 0, which means the projection of the angular momentum on the field axis is zero. This corresponds to the orbital plane containing the field axis. In this orientation, the electron's path would pass through the region of the nucleus (the orbit degenerates such that the electron is on a path that passes through the nucleus). Classically this is forbidden — an electron can't collide with the nucleus in a stable orbit. Hence m ranges over ±1, ±2, ..., ±k only (2k values total).

Larmor's Theorem

Classically, when a charged particle in an orbit is placed in a magnetic field H, its orbit precesses about the field direction — just like a spinning top in gravity. This precession is characterised by the Larmor frequency.

Why does Larmor's Theorem matter?
Larmor's theorem is important in atomic physics because it allows us to compute energy level shifts in an external magnetic field using purely classical electrodynamics, before quantum mechanics was developed. It's also why it plays a central role in AMP (Atomic, Molecular, and Plasma) physics.
Larmor Angular Frequency
ω_L = eH / (2m_e c)
H = magnetic field intensity, e = electron charge, c = speed of light (Gaussian units)

The orbit normal precesses about H at ω_L. This means the energy levels split by amounts proportional to m·ω_L — the normal Zeeman effect.

Important Questions — Lecture 4
▶ What is space quantisation and what is its experimental proof?
Space quantisation is the restriction that the orbital angular momentum vector can only orient itself at certain discrete angles with respect to an external field axis — not continuously. The allowed projections P_φ = mħ give only 2k distinct values (m = ±1,...,±k). This seems bizarre classically. The definitive experimental proof came from the Stern-Gerlach experiment (1922): a beam of silver atoms (with a single valence electron) was passed through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. Classically, we'd expect a continuous spread of deflections. Instead, the beam split into exactly 2 discrete spots — proving space quantisation is real. This also first demonstrated the existence of electron spin (since for orbital quantum number, one would expect an odd number of lines, but Ag gives 2, consistent with spin-1/2).
▶ Derive the energy splitting in a magnetic field using the Bohr magneton.
The additional energy of a magnetic dipole μ in field H is ΔE = −μ·H·cosα, where α is the angle between μ and H. Since P_φ = mħ and μ = (e/2m_e c)P_φ, the z-component is μ_z = (e/2m_e c)·mħ = m·μ_B. Therefore: ΔE = −μ_z · H = −m·μ_B·H = −m·(eħ/2m_e c)·H. Each value of m gives a different energy shift, splitting the original level into 2k sub-levels. This is the normal Zeeman effect. Since m changes by ±1 between levels, the splitting between adjacent sub-levels is Δ(ΔE) = μ_B·H = the Larmor frequency times ħ. This explains why Zeeman splitting is proportional to the applied field strength.

Electron Spin and the Spin Magnetic Moment

The Need for Spin — What Prompted It?

After the Stern-Gerlach experiment and closer analysis of fine-structure doublets, theorists were forced to introduce a fourth quantum number. The key problems that orbital quantum numbers couldn't solve:

  • The Stern-Gerlach experiment on silver atoms showed 2 spots — but silver's outer electron is in a 5s orbital (l=0). Zero orbital angular momentum should give only 1 spot!
  • Spectral lines of alkali metals (Na, K, etc.) were doublets — two closely spaced lines where theory predicted one. E.g., Na yellow D₁ and D₂ lines.
  • The anomalous Zeeman effect showed splitting patterns inconsistent with orbital quantum numbers alone.

Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit (1925) proposed that the electron itself spins about its own axis, like a tiny top. This intrinsic angular momentum is called spin.

Properties of Spin

The electron spin is a purely quantum mechanical property — it has no classical analogue (you cannot model it as a spinning ball of charge). Its key properties:

Spin Quantum Number and Angular Momentum
s = 1/2 (fixed, intrinsic property of the electron)
|S⃗| = √[s(s+1)] · ħ = √(3)/2 · ħ
m_s = ±1/2 (two possible projections: "spin up" and "spin down")
Why only two values for m_s?
For any angular momentum quantum number j, the projection m_j ranges over 2j+1 values from −j to +j. For s = 1/2: 2(1/2)+1 = 2 values → m_s = −1/2 and +1/2. These correspond to spin "up" (↑) and spin "down" (↓) relative to a chosen axis. This is why the Stern-Gerlach experiment on s-orbital electrons gives 2 and only 2 spots.

The Spin Magnetic Moment

Because the electron carries charge and spins, it has a magnetic moment. However, the spin magnetic moment differs from the orbital magnetic moment by a crucial factor: the g-factor.

①
μ_s = (e/2m_e c) · P_s × g_s
General form: orbital analogy scaled by g_s
②
g_s = 2 (experimentally determined; also predicted by Dirac's relativistic QM)
For spin, the gyromagnetic ratio is twice that for orbital motion
③
μ_s = (e/2m_e c) · (2·sħ) = (e/2m_e c) · 2 · (1/2) · ħ = eħ/2m_e c = μ_B
For s = 1/2, the spin magnetic moment equals one Bohr magneton
Spin Magnetic Moment
μ_s = g_s · (e/2m_e c) · |S⃗| = eħ/(2m_e c) = μ_B
μ_s (z-component) = g_s · m_s · μ_B = ± μ_B
⚠ The Factor of 2 — g_s = 2
This is one of the most important and initially mysterious results in atomic physics. For orbital motion, μ_orbital = (e/2m_e c)|L⃗| — a factor of 1. For spin, μ_s = (e/m_e c)|S⃗| — a factor of 2 (i.e. g_s = 2). This anomalous gyromagnetic ratio cannot be derived from classical physics. It is a consequence of the electron being a Dirac fermion — it emerges naturally from Dirac's relativistic wave equation (1928). Quantum electrodynamics (QED) gives g_s = 2.00231... (the anomalous magnetic moment).
Important Questions — Lecture 5
▶ Why can't we calculate the spin magnetic moment classically?
To calculate the magnetic moment classically (as we did for orbital motion), we need precise knowledge of the charge distribution and the shape of the spinning object. For orbital motion, the charge is a point electron in a well-defined orbit — easy. For spin, we would need to know how the charge is distributed within the electron and exactly how it "rotates." Since electrons appear to be point particles (no measurable size), the classical spinning sphere model breaks down completely. Moreover, if you try to model the electron as a spinning sphere with angular momentum ħ/2, the surface would need to spin faster than light — physically impossible. Spin is an intrinsically quantum mechanical property.
▶ Why does the spin contribute twice as much magnetic moment as orbital motion for the same angular momentum?
This is one of the deep results of quantum field theory. Classically, for any continuous charge distribution rotating with angular momentum L, the magnetic moment is μ = (e/2mc)L — the ratio μ/L = e/2mc (the gyromagnetic ratio). Spin behaves differently: its gyromagnetic ratio is e/mc = 2×(e/2mc). This factor of 2 (g_s = 2) is one of the great predictions of Dirac's relativistic quantum mechanics (1928). Physically, spin is not rotation of a charge distribution but an intrinsic relativistic quantum property. The precise value of g_s = 2.002319... (not exactly 2) is due to quantum electrodynamic corrections — and the calculation of this number to 12 significant figures is one of the greatest successes of quantum field theory.

Vector Atom Model — Angular Momentum Coupling

Why a Vector Model?

For multi-electron atoms, each electron has its own orbital angular momentum l⃗ᵢ and spin s⃗ᵢ. These interact magnetically. The vector atom model treats angular momenta as vectors that add according to quantum mechanical rules, enabling us to find the total angular momentum of the atom and predict its spectral properties.

Individual Orbital Angular Momentum (quantum mechanics)
|l⃗| = √[l(l+1)] · ħ
l = 0, 1, 2, ..., (n−1)l labels: s, p, d, f, g, h, ...

The Two Coupling Schemes

For atoms with multiple electrons, the individual angular momenta can couple in two limiting ways, depending on the relative strength of the spin-orbit interaction vs. the electron-electron interaction:

LS Coupling (Russell-Saunders) — Low Z
For light atoms (Z ≲ 30): electron-electron electrostatic repulsion > spin-orbit coupling.

All l⃗ᵢ first add → L⃗ (total orbital AM)
All s⃗ᵢ first add → S⃗ (total spin AM)
Then L⃗ and S⃗ couple → J⃗ = L⃗ + S⃗
L+S+J+m_L+m_S used
jj Coupling — High Z
For heavy atoms (Z ≳ 30): spin-orbit coupling > electron-electron.

Each electron's l⃗ᵢ + s⃗ᵢ → j⃗ᵢ (individual total AM)
All j⃗ᵢ then add → J⃗ = Σ j⃗ᵢ
j₁+j₂+... coupling
L > S vs L < S — Multiplicity
For LS coupling, the number of possible J values is:
  • If L ≥ S: then J = |L−S|, |L−S|+1, ..., L+S → there are (2S+1) values → this is the multiplicity
  • If L < S: then J takes (2L+1) values
The term symbol ²ˢ⁺¹L_J encodes all this information (spectroscopic notation).

Important Questions — Lecture 6
▶ For 3 electrons, write down the coupling procedure in LS coupling.
In LS coupling: (1) Add all orbital angular momenta: L⃗ = l⃗₁ + l⃗₂ + l⃗₃. The total L can range from |l₁−l₂−l₃| to l₁+l₂+l₃, in integer steps. (2) Add all spins: S⃗ = s⃗₁ + s⃗₂ + s⃗₃. For three spin-1/2 electrons, S = 1/2 or 3/2. (3) Couple L and S: J⃗ = L⃗ + S⃗, giving J from |L−S| to L+S. The resulting terms are labeled ²ˢ⁺¹L_J. In jj coupling instead: first j⃗₁ = l⃗₁ + s⃗₁, j⃗₂ = l⃗₂ + s⃗₂, j⃗₃ = l⃗₃ + s⃗₃; then J⃗ = j⃗₁ + j⃗₂ + j⃗₃. The states are labeled with (j₁, j₂, j₃)_J.
▶ What is the maximum number of electrons in a shell with n = 3?
For n = 3, the allowed subshells are l = 0 (3s), l = 1 (3p), l = 2 (3d). Using 2(2l+1): 3s holds 2 electrons, 3p holds 6, 3d holds 10. Total = 2+6+10 = 18 = 2×3² = 2n². Alternatively using the formula: 2Σ(2l+1) from l=0 to 2 = 2(1+3+5) = 2×9 = 18.

Sodium Yellow Lines, Selection Rules, and Spectral Series

Sodium — A Hydrogen-Like (Alkali) Atom

Sodium (Na, Z=11) has configuration 1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹. The inner 10 electrons form a closed core; the valence electron in the 3s orbital behaves almost hydrogen-like, experiencing an effective nuclear charge Z_eff < 11 due to shielding. This is why Na is called "sodium-like" or alkali-like.

Why doublets in Na?
The single valence electron has spin s = 1/2. For any l ≥ 1, the spin-orbit coupling splits the level into j = l+1/2 and j = l−1/2 sub-levels. This produces doublets: e.g., the p-state (l=1) splits into ²P₃/₂ (j=3/2) and ²P₁/₂ (j=1/2). Transitions from both these levels to the ground state ²S₁/₂ give the two D lines of Na.
Fig 7.1 — Energy level diagram for the sodium D lines. The 3p level is split by spin-orbit interaction into ²P₃/₂ and ²P₁/₂. Both can decay to the ²S₁/₂ ground state, giving D₂ (5889.97 Å) and D₁ (5895.94 Å). D₂ is more intense (higher degeneracy of upper level: 2j+1 = 4 vs 2 for D₁).

Selection Rules — The Physical Origin

Not all transitions between energy levels are allowed. Selection rules determine which transitions actually occur (or have significant probability). They arise from the requirement that the transition moment matrix element be non-zero — computed by applying perturbation theory to the radiation-atom interaction.

Transition Moment — Why Selection Rules Arise

The probability of a transition between states ψᵢ and ψ_f is proportional to the square of the transition dipole moment matrix element:

Transition Moment Matrix Element
Rᵢ_f = ⟨ψ_f | r⃗ | ψᵢ⟩ = ∫ ψ_f* · r⃗ · ψᵢ dτ
r⃗ = (x, y, z) is the electric dipole operator. Three components: Rₓ, R_y, R_z.
Transition rate ∝ |Rᵢ_f|²
If Rᵢ_f = 0 in all three components: transition is forbidden (selection rule violated). If any component ≠ 0: transition is allowed.
①
ψ_nlm(r,θ,φ) = R_nl(r) · Y_l^m(θ,φ)
Hydrogen-like wavefunction separates into radial R_nl and spherical harmonic Y_l^m
②
R_z = ∫ R_n'l' Y_l'^m'* · r cosθ · R_nl Y_l^m r² sinθ drdθdφ
z-component: z = r cosθ. Integral separates into radial × angular parts.
③
Angular integral: ∫₀²π e^{i(m−m')φ} dφ = 2π δ_{mm'}
φ-integration of the z-component forces m' = m → Δm = 0 for z (π-transitions)
④
For x ± iy = r sinθ e^{±iφ}: angular integral gives e^{i(m−m'±1)φ} → m' = m ± 1
x and y components (circular combinations) force Δm = ±1 → σ±-transitions
⑤
θ-integral: ∫ Y_l'^m'* cosθ Y_l^m sinθ dθ ≠ 0 only if l' = l ± 1
This uses the recursion relation for associated Legendre polynomials: cosθ P_l^m = combination of P_{l±1}^m. Orthogonality forces l' = l±1, giving Δl = ±1.
✓ Selection Rules from First Principles
The three Cartesian integrals of the transition moment automatically enforce:
  • Δl = ±1 — from the θ-integral (Legendre polynomial orthogonality + parity)
  • Δm = 0 — from the z-integral (φ-integral forces m'=m)
  • Δm = ±1 — from the x,y integrals (φ-integral with e^{±iφ})
  • ΔS = 0 — because the electric dipole operator acts only on spatial coordinates, not spin — spin state is unchanged
Electric Dipole Selection Rules (optical transitions)
Δl = ±1(parity must change)
Δj = ±1, 0(but j=0 → j=0 is forbidden)
Δm_j = ±1, 0(Δm_j = 0 gives π-polarized light; ±1 gives σ)
ΔS = 0(intercombination lines forbidden in LS coupling)
Why Δl = ±1?
A photon has spin 1 (integer). When a photon is emitted or absorbed, angular momentum must be conserved. The orbital angular momentum can change by ±1 (to absorb the photon's angular momentum). Also, the electric dipole operator (the dominant interaction) has odd parity — so the initial and final states must have opposite parity. Since parity = (−1)^l, Δl must be odd → Δl = ±1 (higher changes give magnetic dipole or electric quadrupole, much weaker).

Important Questions — Lecture 7
▶ Why is the D₂ line more intense than D₁ in Na?
The intensity of a spectral line is proportional to the number of atoms in the upper level (population) times the transition probability. At thermal equilibrium, the population is proportional to the degeneracy (2j+1) of the upper level. The ²P₃/₂ level (j=3/2) has degeneracy 2(3/2)+1 = 4. The ²P₁/₂ level (j=1/2) has degeneracy 2(1/2)+1 = 2. So the D₂ transition has roughly twice the intensity of D₁. Also, when the selection rule Δj = +1 (D₂: ²P₃/₂ → ²S₁/₂) is applied, there are more allowed m_j transitions than for Δj = 0 (D₁: ²P₁/₂ → ²S₁/₂), contributing further to the intensity ratio.
▶ Explain what the selection rule ΔS = 0 means physically and when it breaks down.
ΔS = 0 means the spin of the electron doesn't flip during an electric dipole transition. In LS coupling, the spin and orbital degrees of freedom are somewhat independent — the electromagnetic radiation interacts primarily with the orbital (charge distribution) motion, not directly with the spin. So the total spin S must be conserved. This forbids transitions between singlet (S=0) and triplet (S=1) states in, e.g., helium — giving rise to the historical "two element" confusion about helium (parahelium and orthohelium appeared to be separate elements). The rule breaks down when spin-orbit coupling is strong (high-Z elements, jj coupling regime), because L and S are no longer separately good quantum numbers.

The Zeeman Effect — Energy Levels in a Magnetic Field

Normal vs Anomalous Zeeman Effect

When an atom is placed in an external magnetic field, spectral lines split. Two categories:

Normal Zeeman Effect
Each spectral line splits into exactly 3 components. Explained by classical physics (Larmor) and by quantum mechanics when spin is ignored (singlet states, S=0). The splitting is equal to the Larmor frequency.
Anomalous Zeeman Effect
Lines split into more than 3 components, often with unequal spacing. Requires spin and the Landé g-factor to explain. This is the "real" case for most atoms — the normal case is actually the exception (requires S=0).

Energy Splitting in a Magnetic Field

When an atom with total angular momentum J⃗ and magnetic moment μ⃗_J is placed in field B, the potential energy is:

Magnetic Potential Energy (Additional Energy in Field)
ΔE = −μ⃗_J · B⃗ = μ_J B cosθ
ΔE = m_J · g_J · μ_B · B
m_J = magnetic quantum number of J, g_J = Landé g-factor

For the normal Zeeman effect (g_J = 1, ignoring spin), this simplifies to:

Normal Zeeman Splitting
ΔE = m_L · μ_B · B
Levels split by equal amounts of μ_B · B; only m_L changes by Δm_L = 0, ±1
Fig 8.1 — Normal Zeeman effect: a p→s transition (normal Zeeman case). The p level splits into 3 (m_L = −1, 0, +1); s level unchanged (l=0). Three spectral lines observed. σ± (Δm_L = ±1) are circularly polarized when viewed parallel to B, linearly polarized perpendicular when viewed transverse. π (Δm_L = 0) is linearly polarized, absent when viewed parallel to B.

Polarisation of Zeeman Components

σ+ (Δm = +1)
⊥ to B: linear polarisation
∥ to B: left circular (LCP)
π (Δm = 0)
⊥ to B: linear polarisation (∥ to B)
∥ to B: absent
σ− (Δm = −1)
⊥ to B: linear polarisation
∥ to B: right circular (RCP)
Important Questions — Lecture 8
▶ Why does the π component disappear when viewed parallel to the field?
The π component (Δm_J = 0) corresponds to an oscillating electric dipole oriented parallel to the magnetic field B (the z-axis). An oscillating electric dipole radiates in a donut pattern — it emits maximally in directions perpendicular to its axis and zero along its axis. When you view along the field direction (z-axis), you are looking along the dipole's axis, so you observe zero intensity from the π component. For the σ± components (Δm_J = ±1), the dipoles oscillate in the xy plane, and they radiate in all directions including along z — that's why σ components are visible from all angles.

Paschen–Back Effect — Strong Field Limit

When the external magnetic field B is so strong that the Zeeman splitting exceeds the spin-orbit splitting ΔE_SO of the level, the coupling between L⃗ and S⃗ breaks down. L⃗ and S⃗ decouple from J⃗ and each independently precesses about B.

Fig 8.2 — Paschen-Back effect. In a very strong field, L-S coupling breaks: mL and ms become independently quantised. The energy is ΔE = (mL + 2ms)μ_B B. Although there are 6 sub-levels for the p state, the selection rules Δm_L = 0, ±1 and Δm_s = 0 allow only 3 distinct transition energies — restoring the appearance of a normal Zeeman triplet. The pattern is the same but the physical origin is completely different.
Field Regimes Summary
  • No field: Doublet (fine structure from spin-orbit coupling alone). Good quantum numbers: n, l, j, m_j.
  • Weak field (Zeeman): Anomalous splitting. J precesses about B. ΔE = g_J m_J μ_B B. Good quantum numbers: n, l, j, m_j.
  • Strong field (Paschen-Back): L and S independently quantised. ΔE = (m_L + 2m_s) μ_B B. Good quantum numbers: n, l, m_L, m_s. J is no longer good.
  • Very strong field (electric — Stark effect): Analogous breakdown, but due to an electric field.

Anomalous Zeeman Effect and the Landé g-Factor

Why "Anomalous"?

Most atoms (those with S ≠ 0) show more than 3 Zeeman components and unequal spacings. This couldn't be explained by classical theory or Bohr-Sommerfeld theory. The key is that for spin, the gyromagnetic ratio is twice that for orbital motion (g_s = 2). This causes the total magnetic moment μ⃗_J to be not collinear with J⃗ — it points in a different direction.

The Non-collinearity of μ_J and J

Both orbital and spin magnetic moments contribute to the total magnetic moment:

  • μ⃗_L = −(e/2m_e c) L⃗ — drawn twice the L vector in magnitude relative to its unit vector (factor 1)
  • μ⃗_s = −(e/m_e c) S⃗ — drawn 4 times the S vector (factor g_s = 2)

Since g_L = 1 ≠ g_s = 2, the total μ⃗ = μ⃗_L + μ⃗_s does not point along J⃗ = L⃗ + S⃗.

Fig 9.1 — The non-collinearity of μ⃗ and J⃗. Since g_s = 2 ≠ g_L = 1, the total magnetic moment μ⃗ = μ⃗_L + μ⃗_s does not point along J⃗. L⃗ and S⃗ precess around J⃗, and the component of μ⃗ perpendicular to J⃗ averages to zero over one precession period. Only the component μ_J parallel to J⃗ is effective. This effective component is μ_J = g_J · (e/2mc) · |J⃗|.

Deriving the Landé g-Factor

The effective magnetic moment is the time-averaged component of μ⃗ along J⃗. Since L⃗ and S⃗ precess rapidly about J⃗, the components perpendicular to J⃗ average to zero.

①
μ_J = μ_L cos(L,J) + μ_s cos(S,J)
Project μ_L and μ_s onto the J direction
②
μ_L = (e/2mc)|L⃗| = (e/2mc)√(L(L+1))ħ
Orbital magnetic moment in QM
③
μ_s = (e/mc)|S⃗| = (e/mc)√(S(S+1))ħ (g_s=2)
Spin magnetic moment (factor 2 extra)
④
cos(L,J) = [J(J+1)+L(L+1)−S(S+1)] / [2√(J(J+1))√(L(L+1))]
Law of cosines applied to the vector triangle J⃗ = L⃗ + S⃗
⑤
cos(S,J) = [J(J+1)+S(S+1)−L(L+1)] / [2√(J(J+1))√(S(S+1))]
Similarly for the S-J angle
⑥
μ_J = (eħ/2mc)√(J(J+1)) · g_J
Collecting terms; g_J is the Landé g-factor defined below
Landé g-Factor
g_J = 1 + [J(J+1) + S(S+1) − L(L+1)] / [2J(J+1)]
Valid for LS coupling. Reduces to g=1 when S=0 (normal case), g=2 when L=0.

The energy splitting is ΔE = g_J · m_J · μ_B · B. For ²P₃/₂ (g = 4/3): m_J = 3/2, 1/2, −1/2, −3/2 → 4 levels, spacings of (4/3)μ_B·B. The D₁ and D₂ lines each split into multiple components with the pattern determined by allowed transitions (Δm_J = 0, ±1). This is what is actually observed experimentally!

The Stern–Gerlach Experiment — Proof of Space Quantisation

Stern and Gerlach (1922) sent a beam of silver atoms through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. The key:

  • A uniform field would just cause precession (no net force, only torque)
  • An inhomogeneous field exerts a net force: F_z = μ_z · (∂B/∂z)
  • For silver (5s¹, l=0, s=1/2, j=1/2): m_j = ±1/2, giving 2 deflections
  • Classical prediction: continuous spread of deflections
  • Observation: exactly 2 discrete spots → direct proof of space quantisation and spin
⚠ Historical Note on Stern-Gerlach
At the time, the two spots were thought to confirm the spatial quantisation of l (orbital angular momentum). For the silver valence electron in 5s, l=0 should give only 1 spot — the fact that 2 spots appeared was actually the first experimental evidence for spin, though this wasn't recognised until Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit proposed spin in 1925.
Important Questions — Lecture 9
▶ Calculate the Landé g-factor for the state ³D₂.
For ³D₂: S = 1 (triplet, 2S+1=3), L = 2 (D state), J = 2. Using the formula: g_J = 1 + [J(J+1) + S(S+1) − L(L+1)] / [2J(J+1)] = 1 + [2·3 + 1·2 − 2·3] / [2·2·3] = 1 + [6 + 2 − 6] / 12 = 1 + 2/12 = 1 + 1/6 = 7/6. This level will split into 2J+1 = 5 magnetic sub-levels (m_J = −2, −1, 0, +1, +2) with spacing (7/6)μ_B·B — the anomalous Zeeman pattern.
▶ Why is the normal Zeeman effect "normal" — shouldn't spin always be present?
The name is historical — the normal Zeeman effect was discovered first (by Zeeman in 1896, before electron spin was known) and Lorentz explained it classically. It occurs when S = 0 (total spin zero), so g_J = 1 (since the S term vanishes). In this case, the magnetic moment is purely orbital, and the splitting gives exactly 3 lines regardless of L — the "textbook" pattern. In reality, singlet states (S=0) occur only in atoms with even numbers of electrons where spins can pair up (e.g., alkaline earths like Ca, Mg in their singlet excited states). For most atoms and most transitions, S ≠ 0 and the anomalous (more complex) Zeeman pattern is observed. The word "anomalous" is a historical misnomer — the anomalous effect is actually the general case.
▶ What is the Paschen-Back effect?
The Paschen-Back effect occurs when the external magnetic field is so strong that it exceeds the internal spin-orbit coupling field. In this regime, L⃗ and S⃗ decouple from each other and instead each independently precesses about the external field B. The energy becomes ΔE = (m_L + 2m_s)·μ_B·B, with m_L and m_s independently quantised. The anomalous Zeeman pattern simplifies back to a pattern of 3 lines (like the normal Zeeman effect) in this strong-field limit, because the 2m_s and m_L contributions give only 3 possible values for (m_L + 2m_s) between adjacent levels. This was first observed by Paschen and Back in 1912 for lines of Na in strong fields.
